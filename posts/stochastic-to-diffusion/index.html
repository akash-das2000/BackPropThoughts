From Brownian Motion to Discrete Diffusion: The Mathematical Foundations of DDPM

TL;DR

This post traces the mathematical spine of diffusion models, building a clear path from stochastic calculus to practical algorithms:

  1. Brownian motion & Itô calculus. Derive how stochastic processes evolve and prove key identities like \\((dB_t)^2=dt\\).
  2. Fokker–Planck PDE. Show how an SDE induces a PDE over densities \\(p(x,t)\\).
  3. Reverse-time SDE. Step-by-step proof of the reverse drift \\(\\tilde\\mu(x,t)\\) via Anderson (1982) → Song et al. (2020).
  4. Discrete DDPM formulation. Discretize the SDE, derive the forward noising schedule \\(q(x_t\\mid x_0)\\) and posterior \\(q(x_0\\mid x_t)\\).
  5. ELBO and toy DDPM. From variational inference to a minimal Python implementation that visualizes forward/reverse diffusion.

By the end, you'll understand every formula in Ho et al. (2020) as a direct descendant of stochastic calculus.

Introduction

Diffusion models, like those powering state-of-the-art image generation, are built on a deceptively simple idea: progressively corrupt data with Gaussian noise, then learn to reverse that process. But under the hood lies a rich interplay of stochastic calculus, partial differential equations, and variational inference.

This blog post continues our journey from the previous blog on score matching. We now dive deep into the mathematical underpinnings of diffusion models:

  • We start with Brownian motion and show why \\((dB_t)^2=dt\\) is the cornerstone of stochastic integrals.
  • We derive the Fokker–Planck equation from an SDE, revealing how densities \\(p(x,t)\\) evolve over time.
  • We prove the celebrated reverse-time SDE formula that enables denoising in diffusion models.
  • Finally, we bridge to the discrete DDPM formulation, derive its ELBO, and implement a minimal 1D toy model.

Throughout, we provide rigorous proofs, plain-English intuition, and code snippets where applicable, ensuring both theoretical and practical mastery.
