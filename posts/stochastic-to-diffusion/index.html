2. Brownian Motion & Quadratic Variation

2.1 Intuition: Brownian Motion and Quadratic Variation

Brownian motion, or Wiener process, is the cornerstone of stochastic calculus. It describes a continuous-time random walk with independent Gaussian increments:

\[ B_{t+s} - B_t \sim \mathcal{N}(0, s), \quad \text{for all } s \geq 0. \]
  • Independent increments: Each step is independent of the past.
  • Variance scaling: \(\mathrm{Var}[B_t] = t\).

Crucially, Brownian paths are nowhere differentiable but have a well-defined quadratic variation:

\[ (dB_t)^2 = dt. \]

2.2 Proof: Why \((dB_t)^2 = dt\)

Let’s rigorously show how quadratic variation leads to this key identity.

Partition \([0, t]\) into \(n\) intervals of width \(\Delta t = t/n\). The increments:

\[ \Delta B_i = B_{t_i} - B_{t_{i-1}} \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \Delta t). \]

The quadratic variation of \(B_t\) is:

\[ [B]_t = \lim_{n \to \infty} \sum_{i=1}^n (\Delta B_i)^2. \]

Since \(\mathbb{E}[(\Delta B_i)^2] = \Delta t\), the expected sum:

\[ \mathbb{E}\left[\sum_{i=1}^n (\Delta B_i)^2\right] = n\Delta t = t. \]

As \(n \to \infty\), this converges in probability:

\[ [B]_t = t. \]

In differential form, \((dB_t)^2 = dt\) becomes the cornerstone of Itô calculus.

2.3 Connection to Diffusion Models

The identity \((dB_t)^2 = dt\) is not just an abstract feature of Brownian motion; it is the linchpin in the mathematics of diffusion models. It explains how stochasticity enters the forward process and why reversing that process requires special care.

Consider a stochastic differential equation (SDE) of the form:

\[ dx_t = \mu(x_t, t)\,dt + \sigma(t)\,dB_t, \]

where \(\mu(x_t, t)\) is a drift term and \(\sigma(t)\) a diffusion coefficient. To analyze how \(x_t\)'s probability density evolves, we need to consider terms like \((dB_t)^2\) when applying Itô's lemma. Here’s why:

  • First-order differentials: \(dB_t\) contributes random noise.
  • Second-order terms: Due to the quadratic variation, \((dB_t)^2\) contributes a deterministic \(dt\) term.

Without \((dB_t)^2=dt\), the derivation of the Fokker–Planck equation (Section 3) and the reverse-time SDE (Section 4) would break down. This identity ensures second derivatives survive in the limiting process, leading to diffusion in probability space.

In diffusion models, the forward process is a discretization of such SDEs. Each noise injection step (see Eq. (1.1) in Section 1) approximates the continuous Brownian-driven evolution. Later, reversing this noisy evolution relies on knowing how \((dB_t)^2\) contributes to drift corrections.

Key Takeaway: The seemingly innocuous \((dB_t)^2=dt\) bridges random fluctuations to smooth deterministic effects in density evolution. It is the technical reason why adding noise step-by-step produces Gaussian marginals.
