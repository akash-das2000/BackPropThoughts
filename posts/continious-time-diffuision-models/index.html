Continuous-Time Diffusion Models: SDE Formulations and Variational Objectives

TL;DR

This post extends our mathematical journey into diffusion models, moving beyond discrete timesteps to a continuous-time framework:

  1. Continuous SDEs: Generalize discrete forward/reverse processes to stochastic differential equations of the form dx_t = f(x, t) dt + g(t) dB_t.
  2. Noise Schedules: Map variance schedules beta(t) to drift and diffusion coefficients.
  3. Variational Objectives: Derive a continuous-time ELBO using Girsanov’s theorem and connect it to the Fisher information integral.
  4. Score Matching: Interpret the loss as continuous-time score matching for learning nabla_x log p_t(x).
  5. Outlook: Preview Predictor–Corrector samplers and deterministic probability flow ODEs for faster sampling.

By the end, you’ll see how Song et al. (2021)’s framework unifies diffusion models under elegant continuous-time dynamics.

Introduction

In our previous post, we traced the mathematical spine of diffusion models, from Brownian motion and Itô calculus to the discrete Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Models (DDPM) framework. We derived the forward and reverse stochastic differential equations (SDEs), explored their connection to the Fokker–Planck PDE, and built up to the variational ELBO that makes DDPMs trainable in practice.

While discrete diffusion models like DDPM are powerful, their reliance on fixed timesteps can be limiting. This motivates the transition to continuous-time diffusion models, where stochastic processes evolve in an unbroken time interval and neural networks learn to approximate the score function across all t ∈ [0, T].

In this post, we build on the foundations laid earlier and dive into the continuous-time perspective. We will derive forward and reverse SDEs, map noise schedules β(t) to drift and diffusion coefficients, and show how Girsanov’s theorem leads naturally to a continuous-time ELBO. Along the way, we will see how the Fisher information emerges in this setting and why score-based generative models are a natural generalization of DDPMs.
